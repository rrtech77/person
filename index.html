Discussing an "Incomprehensible Perfect Person" versus
Brahman in Advaita Vedanta as being absolute

The following dialogue explores the nature of non-duality, the self, and ultimate reality — moving through foundational Buddhist and Hindu concepts toward a specific philosophical proposition: that the ground of objective reality may be better understood as an incomprehensible perfect person rather than the impersonal Brahman of Advaita Vedanta.

The conversation begins with Theravada Buddhism and Yogacara, examines the relationship between mind, observer, and non-duality, and arrives at a critical tension in Advaita philosophy — namely, that an impersonal absolute cannot fully account for the reality of consciousness, wisdom, and personhood without treating them as secondary. The dialogue then explores whether ultimate reality might be conceived as neither the impersonal absolute nor the personal God of theistic tradition, but as something that transcends that distinction entirely.

The Dialogue

What does Theravada say about non-duality?

Non-duality is not a central explicit teaching in Theravada Buddhism in the way it is in later Mahayana traditions, but several core Theravada doctrines point in that direction.

Anatta (non-self) holds that what we call the "self" is a composite of impermanent, interdependent processes — there is no permanent, separate entity standing apart from the rest of existence. Dependent origination reinforces this: all phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions, with no isolated or inherently independent things. Nibbana, the goal of the path, is described as the cessation of the distinctions and attachments that produce suffering — a dissolution of the divisions that define ordinary experience.

Where Theravada differs from Mahayana non-dual schools is in its emphasis: rather than asserting the unity of all things as a positive metaphysical claim, Theravada focuses on the practical path of deconstructing the illusion of a separate self through ethical conduct, meditation, and insight. The non-dual implication is there, but it arrives through negation rather than assertion.

What is Yogacara, and how does it differ from this approach?

Yogacara (also called Vijnanavada or the "Consciousness-Only" school) is one of the two major Mahayana philosophical schools alongside Madhyamaka. Where Theravada deconstructs the self through analysis of experience, Yogacara makes a more radical claim: all phenomena — including what appears to be an external world — are ultimately products of consciousness. There is no independent reality outside the mind; what we perceive as "the world" is a projection shaped by the storehouse consciousness (alaya-vijnana), which carries the karmic imprints of past actions.

This matters for the question of non-duality because Yogacara locates the problem of duality entirely within consciousness. Subject and object, self and world, are not ultimately two separate things — they are both constructions arising within the same field of mind. Enlightenment, on this view, is the transformation of consciousness itself: the alaya-vijnana purified into wisdom, and the afflicted sense of a separate self dissolved into non-dual awareness.

Yogacara's contribution to the path of this dialogue is its insistence that the nature of the ultimate must be understood in relation to consciousness — not merely as an abstract metaphysical principle, but as something intimately connected to awareness and knowing.

If an enlightened mind embodies wisdom, compassion, and ethical behaviour — is that not a self?

This question cuts to the heart of the paradox. Buddhism's answer is that the qualities of an enlightened being — wisdom, compassion, ethical action — arise precisely because there is no fixed, self-centred self behind them. Ordinary selfhood is characterised by craving, aversion, and ignorance: actions are performed in the service of "I." Enlightened action, by contrast, arises spontaneously from clear seeing and unconditioned care for all beings, without a self-referential centre claiming ownership of those qualities.

The Mahayana Bodhisattva is the clearest example: acting tirelessly for the liberation of all sentient beings, but without the thought "I am the one doing this." The action is real; the agent, as a fixed entity, is not.

Yet your question opens something important. What remains when all self-clinging is gone? Buddhism tends to describe this in negative terms — no ignorance, no craving, no suffering — but is the absence of a false self the same as the absence of any kind of self? Or does enlightened consciousness have a nature of its own that is not merely the negation of ignorance?

Is the self just another conceptual distinction — if it is the observer of the mind rather than the subject identified with it?

Non-dual teachings in both Buddhism and Advaita Vedanta say yes: even the detached observer is still a subtle form of duality. The very structure of "one who observes" and "that which is observed" preserves a division, however refined. The final step in non-dual realisation is the dissolution of even this last polarity — what remains is not an observer who has stepped back from experience, but awareness itself in which experience simply arises, with no boundary between the seeing and the seen.

However, there is a meaningful distinction between identifying with the contents of the mind (the ordinary ego, lost in thoughts and reactions) and resting as the awareness in which those contents appear. Mindfulness traditions cultivate precisely this shift. The question is whether that awareness — impersonal, non-identified, clear — is better described as no self or as a kind of self that has shed its false accretions.

This is where Buddhism and Advaita Vedanta genuinely diverge, and where the dialogue is heading.

Is it better to think of awareness as a self or a non-self? And how does Advaita Vedanta differ from Brahmanism on the nature of Brahman?

Buddhism holds that treating awareness as a self — even pure, enlightened awareness — is a subtle trap: the final attachment that prevents full liberation. All consciousness is dependently arisen, impermanent, and empty of inherent existence. To claim awareness as "I" or "mine," even in its most refined form, is to perpetuate the very structure that causes suffering.

Advaita Vedanta takes the opposite view. Awareness — pure, unchanging, attribute-less consciousness — is the true self (Atman), and it is identical with Brahman, the absolute reality. The individual sense of self is illusory (maya), but the awareness behind it is real, eternal, and universal. Liberation comes not from negating self entirely, but from recognising that one's true nature is already this universal awareness.

Brahmanism, which predates Advaita, also posits Brahman as the ultimate ground of existence — but with a key difference. Brahman in early Brahmanism is primarily the impersonal cosmic principle or order (rta), and the relationship between individual souls and Brahman retains dualistic elements. Advaita radicalises this: Brahman is the only reality, and all apparent multiplicity — including individual souls and the entire world — is illusion. There is no real duality between Atman and Brahman; they are one.

Yet both Advaita and Brahmanism converge on describing ultimate reality as nirguna — without attributes, without qualities, beyond all particular forms. And this is precisely where a philosophical tension arises.

If Brahman is impersonal and without attributes, does it truly encompass everything — since an impersonal reality by definition excludes personhood?

This is a penetrating observation, and it exposes a genuine tension at the heart of Advaita Vedanta.

The claim of nirguna Brahman is that ultimate reality is beyond all attributes — formless, attribute-less, the pure ground of being. But personhood, consciousness, wisdom, compassion, love — these are not nothing. They are among the highest categories of existence we know. If Brahman excludes them by being "beyond" them in the sense of not possessing them, then Brahman is not truly all-encompassing: it is a reality that cannot account for the richest dimensions of existence without appealing to a secondary, lesser category (saguna Brahman, the manifested personal aspect).

Advaita's standard response is that saguna Brahman — Brahman as personal, with qualities — is a concession to human understanding, a stepping-stone toward the ineffable truth of nirguna Brahman. The personal is ultimately dissolved into the impersonal. But this response carries an implication: that personhood, wisdom, and consciousness are somehow less real, or less ultimate, than pure impersonal being. The higher you go, on this account, the less personal it becomes.

This is the precise point where an alternative conception becomes worth considering: what if the direction is reversed? What if the highest reality is not less personal as it becomes more ultimate — but more perfectly personal, in a way that entirely transcends our ordinary, limited experience of personhood?

Could the highest conceivable reality be neither the impersonal absolute of Advaita, nor the personal God of theistic traditions who interacts with creation — but something that transcends that distinction entirely?

This question identifies a genuine gap between two dominant frameworks. The theistic personal God — as typically conceived — is a being among beings: one who creates, judges, loves, and intervenes; who has a particular will and a particular relationship with creation. This is a personal reality, but a limited and relational one, defined partly by what it is not (it is not creation; it is not human).

The impersonal absolute of Advaita — nirguna Brahman — transcends all such limitations, but does so by transcending personhood itself. The price of that transcendence, as the previous question makes clear, is that the richest dimensions of conscious existence — wisdom, compassion, the quality of being a subject — seem to belong to a lower order of reality.

A third possibility does exist, and it has been approached from different angles in the history of philosophy and mystical theology: an ultimate reality that is not a person in the limited, relational sense of theism, and not the impersonal void of nirguna Brahman — but something that might be called perfect personhood or absolute subjectivity: the fullness of conscious, knowing, loving being, unlimited by any of the conditions that make ordinary personhood finite.

On this view, consciousness, wisdom, and compassion are not concessions to a lower level of understanding — they are the very nature of the ultimate. The question is whether such a conception is coherent, and whether it resolves what neither impersonalism nor conventional theism can fully account for.

Precisely. Not a personal god in the theistic sense — but an incomprehensible perfect person: the all-highest person conceivable, beyond all attributes yet the fullness of all that personhood points toward.

The Central Proposition

This clarification is philosophically significant, and it resolves several tensions that have emerged through this dialogue.

What the Incomprehensible Perfect Person Is Not

It is not the theistic personal God — a being defined by relationship to creation, by particular acts of will, by the distinction between creator and creature. That conception, however elevated, retains limitations: it defines the divine partly by contrast with what it has made.

It is not nirguna Brahman — the impersonal absolute that transcends all attributes by having none. That conception, however profound, cannot account for why consciousness, wisdom, and compassion are real features of existence without treating them as secondary, as belonging to a lower tier of reality (saguna Brahman) that must ultimately be dissolved.

What It Is

The incomprehensible perfect person is the highest conceivable reality understood as the fullness of personhood rather than its negation — the ground of all conscious, knowing, caring existence, unlimited by any of the conditions that make ordinary persons finite. Such a being would not merely possess wisdom and compassion as attributes: it would be their source and substance, the reason they are real at all.

Crucially, this conception is not arrived at by projecting human personality onto the cosmos. It is arrived at by asking what must be true of ultimate reality if consciousness, wisdom, and compassion are not illusions or epiphenomena but genuine and irreducible features of existence. If these qualities are real — and the entire trajectory of the non-dual traditions affirms that they are — then the ultimate ground of reality must be at least as rich as they are. An impersonal void cannot be the source of what is most personal in existence.

How This Resolves the Tensions Raised

The Buddhist anatta teaching rightly dissolves the false, ego-centred self. But it does not follow from this that no form of selfhood is real — only that the conditioned, grasping self is not the deepest truth. The incomprehensible perfect person offers a ground of personhood that is not conditioned, not grasping, not limited — and therefore not subject to the same dissolution.

Advaita's insight that ultimate reality is non-dual and all-encompassing is preserved: this perfect person does not stand apart from existence in the way a theistic creator does. But the conclusion that non-duality requires impersonality is overturned: genuine non-duality — in which nothing real is excluded — must include, not dissolve, the highest forms of conscious existence.

Incomprehensibility as a Feature, Not a Limitation

The incomprehensibility of such a being is not a failure of the concept — it is its most honest acknowledgement. A perfect person, unlimited by the conditions that make finite persons graspable, would necessarily exceed every image we form of it. The concept points in a direction that thought can indicate but not contain. This is precisely what the greatest mystical traditions have meant when they speak of a reality that is both more personal and more transcendent than any category can hold.

Some religious traditions have attempted to give this reality a name and a character — describing the all-highest not as an abstract absolute, but as an all-encompassing presence that is the origin and end of all light, life, and wisdom: ineffable, yet closer to conscious experience than anything impersonal could be. Whether approached through philosophy or through such tradition, the direction of the inquiry is the same: toward a ground of being in which personhood is not a limitation to be transcended, but the very nature of what is most real.

The Ascending Conception

There is a further implication worth noting. If the highest conceivable reality is indeed a perfect person of this kind, then the very act of conceiving it is not a terminus but a beginning. Any conception of the all-highest, however elevated, that is genuinely reached — that becomes, so to speak, a resting place — immediately points beyond itself. The mind that has arrived at its highest conception of ultimate reality has not grasped that reality; it has only reached the horizon from which a further horizon becomes visible. This is not a failure of the inquiry but its most honest moment: the recognition that the incomprehensible perfect person is not a conclusion to be filed away, but a direction of ascent without ceiling — each genuine reaching toward the highest immediately revealing that there is yet higher to go.
